# The Hunter Jesus Hunted Down

## Lesson Brief

- **Series:** Adventures in the Bible
- **Subject/Theme:** Jesus seeks and saves even the worst of sinners.
- **Bible Focus:** Saul's persecution of Christians and his conversion on the road to Damascus.
- **Main Passage:** Acts 7:54-60; Acts 8:1-3; Acts 9:1-22
- **Main Point:** No one has sinned too badly to be saved; Jesus came to seek and save sinners and can completely change anyone who believes on Him.

## Possible Lesson Titles

- The Hunter Jesus Hunted Down
- Stopped On The Damascus Road
- From Enemy To Preacher

## Memory Verse Options

- **1 Timothy 1:15** - "This is a faithful saying, and worthy of all acceptation, that Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners; of whom I am chief." Paul used his own past to show how willing Jesus is to save sinners.
- **Luke 19:10** - "For the Son of man is come to seek and to save that which was lost." This captures the lesson's central truth: while Saul was hunting Christians, Jesus was seeking Saul.
- **Luke 5:31-32** - "They that are whole need not a physician; but they that are sick. I came not to call the righteous, but sinners to repentance." Jesus did not avoid sinful people; He came to call them to repentance.

## Lesson Outline

### 1. Saul Stood With The Men Who Killed Stephen

**Main Passage:** Acts 7:54-60; Acts 8:1

> **Acts 7:58**
>
> And cast him out of the city, and stoned him: and the witnesses laid down their clothes at a young man's feet, whose name was Saul.

> **Acts 8:1**
>
> And Saul was consenting unto his death. And at that time there was a great persecution against the church which was at Jerusalem; and they were all scattered abroad throughout the regions of Judaea and Samaria, except the apostles.

**Summary/Lesson:** Begin by introducing Stephen, a man who loved Jesus and boldly told the truth. Angry men dragged him outside Jerusalem and threw stones at him until he died. Stephen prayed for the Lord to receive him, and he asked God not to lay this sin to the charge of the men killing him.

Nearby stood a young man named Saul. The witnesses laid their coats at his feet while the stones flew. Saul did not throw the stones, as far as Scripture tells us, but he approved of what they were doing. Acts 8:1 says that Saul was "consenting unto his death." Later, Saul remembered that moment and admitted, "I also was standing by, and consenting unto his death" (Acts 22:20). Saul watched a Christian die and believed it was right.

Do not rush past this scene. Let the children feel the danger facing the first Christians. Stephen was killed for preaching Jesus, and Saul stood with the people who killed him. This is the first picture the Bible gives us of the man who would later be called Paul.

**Supporting Scriptures:** Acts 22:19-20 records Saul's later confession that he imprisoned and beat believers and consented to Stephen's death. Acts 26:9 says Saul once thought he ought to do many things contrary to the name of Jesus of Nazareth.

**Practical Application for Children:** Going along with someone else's sin is still wrong. A child may not start the cruel joke, throw the first punch, or tell the first lie, but cheering it on or helping it happen puts that child on the wrong side. We should stand with Jesus even when other people are against Him.

**Illustration:** Ask one child to hold several coats while an imaginary angry crowd moves past. Explain that Saul may have looked like only a coat-holder, but his heart was fully on the side of the killers. What we support reveals what is in our hearts.

### 2. Saul Hunted Christians From House To House

**Main Passage:** Acts 8:1-3

> **Acts 8:3**
>
> As for Saul, he made havock of the church, entering into every house, and haling men and women committed them to prison.

**Summary/Lesson:** Stephen's death was not enough for Saul. A great persecution began, and believers fled from Jerusalem. Imagine Christian families listening for footsteps outside their doors. Imagine parents trying to keep their children quiet when someone pounded on the door. Saul entered house after house, dragged away both men and women, and had them put in prison.

The Bible says Saul "made havock of the church." He was trying to tear it apart. In his later testimony, Saul said, "I persecuted this way unto the death, binding and delivering into prisons both men and women" (Acts 22:4). He also said that he punished believers, tried to force them to blaspheme, and persecuted them even unto strange cities (Acts 26:11). Saul was not merely confused or impolite. He was a violent enemy of Christ and His people.

**Supporting Scriptures:** Acts 22:3-5 describes Saul's zeal and his imprisonment of believers. Acts 26:9-11 says he shut up saints in prison, gave his voice against them when they were put to death, punished them in synagogues, and pursued them to other cities. Galatians 1:13 says he "persecuted the church of God, and wasted it."

**Practical Application for Children:** Sin hurts real people. Anger, cruelty, hateful words, and bullying are not small things just because someone feels strongly or thinks he has a good reason. We must let God's Word show us what is right instead of letting pride or anger control us.

**Illustration:** Tear a scrap sheet of paper into several pieces while explaining the word "havock." Saul wanted to scatter Christian families and tear the church apart. Keep the torn pieces nearby to use again after Saul's conversion.

### 3. Saul Set Out To Capture Even More Believers

**Main Passage:** Acts 9:1-2

> **Acts 9:1-2**
>
> And Saul, yet breathing out threatenings and slaughter against the disciples of the Lord, went unto the high priest, And desired of him letters to Damascus to the synagogues, that if he found any of this way, whether they were men or women, he might bring them bound unto Jerusalem.

**Summary/Lesson:** Saul heard that there were followers of Jesus in Damascus, more than one hundred miles away. That was not too far for him. He went to the high priest and asked for official letters giving him authority to arrest them. Then he started down the road with companions, determined to return with Christian men and women as prisoners.

Build the tension here. Every step brought Saul closer to Damascus. Perhaps the believers there did not yet know he was coming. Saul had authority in his hand, anger in his heart, and a plan in his mind. He thought he was the hunter. But there was Someone Saul had not planned to meet on that road.

**Supporting Scriptures:** Acts 22:5 says Saul received letters and went to Damascus to bring believers bound to Jerusalem. Acts 26:12 says he traveled with authority and commission from the chief priests. Proverbs 16:9 reminds us, "A man's heart deviseth his way: but the LORD directeth his steps."

**Practical Application for Children:** A person can be sincere and still be terribly wrong. Saul felt sure of himself, but his strong feelings did not make his actions right. Children need to compare what they think and feel with what God has said in His Word.

**Illustration:** Show an envelope labeled "Damascus" and explain that Saul carried permission to arrest Christians. Ask, "If Saul had written the ending of this trip, what would have happened? But who was really in control of that road?"

### 4. Jesus Stopped Saul On The Road

**Main Passage:** Acts 9:3-9

> **Acts 9:3-6**
>
> And as he journeyed, he came near Damascus: and suddenly there shined round about him a light from heaven: And he fell to the earth, and heard a voice saying unto him, Saul, Saul, why persecutest thou me? And he said, Who art thou, Lord? And the Lord said, I am Jesus whom thou persecutest: it is hard for thee to kick against the pricks. And he trembling and astonished said, Lord, what wilt thou have me to do? And the Lord said unto him, Arise, and go into the city, and it shall be told thee what thou must do.

**Summary/Lesson:** Just as Saul came near Damascus, a light from heaven suddenly shone around him. It was brighter than the midday sun (Acts 26:13). Saul fell to the ground. Then he heard a voice call his own name: "Saul, Saul, why persecutest thou me?"

Saul asked, "Who art thou, Lord?" The answer must have struck him harder than the light: "I am Jesus whom thou persecutest." Jesus was alive. Stephen had told the truth. The Christians Saul arrested had told the truth. Saul had believed he was fighting a false religion, but he had actually been fighting the risen Son of God. When Saul hurt Christ's people, Jesus took it personally: "Why persecutest thou me?"

This is the climax of the story. Saul was coming to Damascus to find Christians, but Jesus found Saul first. Jesus knew every cruel thing Saul had done. He knew about Stephen, the homes, the prisons, the threats, and the deaths. Yet Jesus did not say Saul was too wicked to save. Jesus stopped him, confronted him with the truth, and called him to a completely new life.

Saul arose from the ground, but he could not see. The powerful man who planned to lead prisoners back to Jerusalem now had to be led by the hand into Damascus. For three days he could neither see nor eat nor drink. Saul had entered that road proud and threatening; he entered Damascus blind, humbled, and waiting on God.

**Supporting Scriptures:** Acts 22:6-11 and Acts 26:12-18 give Saul's later accounts of the heavenly light and Jesus' words. Luke 19:10 says, "For the Son of man is come to seek and to save that which was lost." Luke 5:31-32 says Jesus came to call sinners to repentance. Romans 5:8 teaches that Christ died for us "while we were yet sinners."

**Practical Application for Children:** Jesus already knows every sin you have committed, including the sins no one else knows about. You do not need to hide from Him or try to make yourself good enough first. Jesus came looking for lost sinners. Admit the truth about your sin, turn to Him, and believe on Him to save you.

**Illustration:** Turn off the room lights briefly, then switch on a bright flashlight pointed safely toward the floor or wall. The sudden change can help the children imagine Saul's surprise. Explain that the greatest shock was not the brightness—it was discovering that Jesus was alive and that Saul had been fighting Him.

### 5. Jesus Sent Ananias To His Frightened Enemy

**Main Passage:** Acts 9:10-17

> **Acts 9:13-15**
>
> Then Ananias answered, Lord, I have heard by many of this man, how much evil he hath done to thy saints at Jerusalem: And here he hath authority from the chief priests to bind all that call on thy name. But the Lord said unto him, Go thy way: for he is a chosen vessel unto me, to bear my name before the Gentiles, and kings, and the children of Israel:

> **Acts 9:17**
>
> And Ananias went his way, and entered into the house; and putting his hands on him said, Brother Saul, the Lord, even Jesus, that appeared unto thee in the way as thou camest, hath sent me, that thou mightest receive thy sight, and be filled with the Holy Ghost.

**Summary/Lesson:** In Damascus lived a disciple named Ananias. The Lord told him where Saul was and commanded him to go. Ananias knew Saul's reputation. He had heard how much evil Saul had done, and he knew Saul had come to arrest believers like him. His fear was reasonable—but Jesus told him to go.

When Ananias entered the house, he did something remarkable. He put his hands on the former persecutor and called him "Brother Saul." Jesus had a purpose for the man everyone else knew as an enemy. Saul would carry Christ's name to Gentiles, kings, and Israel. The man who had tried to silence the name of Jesus would spend the rest of his life proclaiming it.

**Supporting Scriptures:** Acts 22:12-16 tells how Ananias instructed Saul to arise and call upon the name of the Lord. Acts 26:16-18 describes Jesus sending Saul to open people's eyes and turn them from darkness to light. First Timothy 1:12-16 says Paul obtained mercy so his life could display Christ's longsuffering as an example to others who would believe.

**Practical Application for Children:** Do not decide that another person is beyond God's reach. The mean child, the bully, the angry neighbor, or the person with a terrible reputation still needs Jesus. We should be wise about danger, but we can pray for people, tell them the gospel when we safely can, and believe that Jesus is able to save them.

**Illustration:** Ask how Ananias might have felt walking toward the house where Saul waited. With each imaginary step, repeat one thing Ananias knew: "He arrested Christians. He came for people like me. He has authority." Then read Jesus' words: "Go thy way: for he is a chosen vessel unto me."

### 6. The Enemy Of Jesus Became A Preacher Of Jesus

**Main Passage:** Acts 9:18-22

> **Acts 9:18-20**
>
> And immediately there fell from his eyes as it had been scales: and he received sight forthwith, and arose, and was baptized. And when he had received meat, he was strengthened. Then was Saul certain days with the disciples which were at Damascus. And straightway he preached Christ in the synagogues, that he is the Son of God.

**Summary/Lesson:** Immediately Saul received his sight. He arose, was baptized, and spent time with the very disciples he had intended to arrest. Then he entered the synagogues—but not to hunt Christians. He preached that Jesus is the Son of God. Everyone who heard him was amazed. They knew what Saul had done in Jerusalem and why he had come to Damascus.

Jesus did not merely improve Saul's manners or calm down his temper. Jesus gave him a new life, a new family, and a new purpose. Saul's past was still real, and he never pretended otherwise. Years later he called himself the chief of sinners, but he also joyfully said, "Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners" (1 Timothy 1:15). Saul's terrible past became evidence of Christ's astonishing mercy.

Bring back the torn paper from Point 2. The pieces cannot repair themselves. Explain that we cannot undo our sin or make our own hearts new. Jesus alone saves. If desired, place the pieces beside a clean sheet labeled "a new creature" and quote 2 Corinthians 5:17. The clean sheet does not mean Saul had never sinned; it pictures the new life Christ gave him.

**Supporting Scriptures:** Second Corinthians 5:17 says that anyone in Christ is a new creature. First Timothy 1:12-16 shows Paul praising Christ for saving and using a former blasphemer and persecutor. Galatians 1:23 records believers hearing, "He which persecuted us in times past now preacheth the faith which once he destroyed."

**Practical Application for Children:** Salvation produces a changed direction. A saved child is not instantly perfect, but Jesus gives that child a new heart and a desire to follow Him. If you are saved, do not let your past convince you that God cannot use you. Admit your wrong, make things right where possible, and begin obeying Jesus today.

**Illustration:** Draw two large arrows pointing in opposite directions. Label one "Fighting Jesus" and the other "Following Jesus." Saul was traveling toward Damascus to fight Christ's people; after meeting Jesus, he began proclaiming Christ. Conversion means Jesus changes the direction of a life.

## Segue Into The Plan Of Salvation

Saul's story is dramatic, but the reason he needed to be saved is the same reason every one of us needs to be saved. Saul had frightening sins in his past, while a child may think, "I have never put anyone in prison." But the Bible says, "For all have sinned, and come short of the glory of God" (Romans 3:23). We have lied, disobeyed, acted selfishly, harbored anger, and chosen our way instead of God's way. Sin makes every person lost and separates us from God.

Jesus said, "They that are whole need not a physician; but they that are sick" (Luke 5:31). A sick person does not prove he is well before visiting a doctor; he goes because he needs help. Jesus did not come because people were already righteous. He said, "I came not to call the righteous, but sinners to repentance" (Luke 5:32). We must stop defending our sin and agree with God that we need to be saved.

Jesus, the sinless Son of God, died on the cross for our sins and rose again. Saul later wrote, "Christ died for our sins according to the scriptures; And that he was buried, and that he rose again the third day according to the scriptures" (1 Corinthians 15:3-4). The risen Jesus who met Saul is alive today.

No history is so bad that the blood of Jesus cannot cleanse it. No child is so good that he does not need Jesus. Salvation is not earned by becoming nicer, getting baptized, going to church, or fixing everything first. "Believe on the Lord Jesus Christ, and thou shalt be saved" (Acts 16:31). Turn from trusting yourself and believe that Jesus died and rose again to save you. If you want to be saved, speak with a teacher after the lesson so someone can show you from the Bible how to trust Christ.

## Main Applications For The Kids

- **If you are not saved, come to Jesus today.** He already knows your whole history, He came to seek and save the lost, and He will save anyone who believes on Him.
- **Do not call anyone too bad for Jesus to save.** Pray for difficult people and, when it is safe and appropriate, tell them what Jesus has done.
- **If you are saved, live in your new direction.** You will not become perfect overnight, but you can confess sin, obey Christ, and let others see that Jesus is changing you.
- **Do not let past sin keep you from serving God.** Saul never bragged about his past, but he also did not let it stop him from obeying Christ. God's mercy is greater than your history.

## Optional Games

- **Damascus Road Relay:** Divide the children into teams. One child is gently blindfolded while a teammate gives spoken directions through a simple, safe path. Switch roles until everyone has participated. Connect it to Saul needing others to lead him by the hand after Jesus stopped him.
- **Who Said It?** Read short statements from Stephen, Saul, Jesus, and Ananias in Acts 7-9. Teams earn a point by identifying the speaker and an extra point by explaining where the statement belongs in the story.
- **Before And After:** Prepare cards describing parts of Saul's life, such as "guarded the coats," "entered houses," "traveled to Damascus," "was led by the hand," and "preached Christ." Children race to place the cards in order and sort them under "Before Jesus Stopped Him" and "After Jesus Saved Him."
